# Right-Sizing Cowork: Who Actually Needs It

## What This Is

A field perspective for the conversation every IT leader is having right now: if Cowork is more expensive to run, who do we actually give it to? The short answer is that you don't roll it out by headcount, you scope it by work shape. This page gives you the framework I use to separate the people who genuinely need Cowork from the much larger group that Copilot Premium already covers, plus a slide deck you can share.

> [!NOTE]
> This is written IT-manager to IT-manager, not as a sales pitch. The most credible move in this conversation is telling people who should *not* get Cowork.

## Start by Fixing the Math, Not Answering It

The first question is always some version of "if we give this to all our users, how many use it and what does it cost." That's the wrong unit of measurement. Nobody should be sizing Cowork by headcount. You size it by work shape.

The fear is a large seat count times some scary per-user number. The reality is that only a slice of those people do work that Cowork is actually built for. Everyone else gets most of the value from Copilot Premium in a couple extra steps. So the real cost question isn't "what if everyone uses it," it's "who are the few hundred people whose work is genuinely project-shaped, and what are they worth." That reframe alone usually drops the anxiety, because you're no longer underwriting an unbounded number.

---

## The One Question That Separates Premium From Cowork

Here's the test I give people. For any user, ask:

> Does this person repeatedly do work that is **multi-step, spans multiple sources, and ends in a structured deliverable** they currently assemble by hand?

- If **no** (their use is in-the-moment: summarize this thread, draft this reply, catch me up, clean up this doc), **Premium already covers them.** Cowork would mostly idle.
- If **yes** (they kick off something on Monday that pulls from five places and produces a report, deck, model, or package), **that's a Cowork person.** The agentic, runs-longer, does-the-stitching behavior is exactly the part Premium makes you do manually.

Cowork costs more per task because it runs longer and does more. That only pays off when it's replacing expensive human assembly hours, not when it's answering a one-shot question Premium handles fine.

## Who the Cowork Personas Actually Are

These are work patterns, not job titles. Title is a bad proxy. The work shape is the signal.

| Persona | Why Cowork fits | Why Premium isn't enough |
|---|---|---|
| **Analysts and researchers** (market, financial, technical) | Synthesize many sources into one report on a cycle | The multi-source stitching is the whole job |
| **FP&A and finance** | Recurring reporting, reconciliation, variance narratives | Repetitive multi-step work that recurs monthly |
| **Program and ops managers** | Coordinate across systems, produce status and tracking docs | Output is a maintained deliverable, not a chat answer |
| **Consultants and pre-sales** | Build proposals, briefs, decks from scattered inputs | The deliverable assembly is the time sink |
| **Legal, compliance, contract review** | Multi-document review and summarization at volume | Volume and structure exceed in-flow assistance |
| **Chiefs of staff and exec support** | Build prep packages and briefings from many feeds | Recurring, multi-source, deliverable-shaped |
| **Marketing and content production** | Multi-asset campaigns from a single brief | Produces a set of artifacts, not one reply |

The dense pockets are usually research and analytics, finance, compliance and contracting, and the PMO. Not the bulk of frontline or task workers. Every org has its own version of these, so the exercise is to map the pattern onto your own population rather than assume a department.

## Who Does NOT Need It (And You Should Say This Out Loud)

- Frontline and task workers
- Anyone whose use is "help me with this thing right now"
- Light email, meeting, and doc users
- People who already have a domain-specific agent, like engineers with coding agents

Telling people who should not get it is the most credible thing you can do. It's the opposite of the sales motion, and it's why they'll trust the rest.

---

## How an Organization Actually Picks (A Method, Not a Vibe)

Don't hand-wave "we'll just figure out who." Use a real selection path.

1. **Tier the population first.** Three buckets: Consumers (base assistant), Assisted (Premium), Builders (Cowork). The Builders tier should feel uncomfortably small at first. That's correct.
2. **Use signals, not nominations.** Look at who already burns Premium heavily, who runs long multi-step prompts, who owns recurring deliverables. Managers nominating their whole team is noise. Usage data is signal.
3. **Run a cohort, not a rollout.** Start with 50 to 150 of the clearest Builders. Measure hours saved per deliverable, deliverables produced, and what they stopped doing manually.
4. **Set a payback threshold up front.** Decide before the pilot what "worth it" means, like X hours saved per user per month or N recurring deliverables automated. Then expand only into people who clear that bar.
5. **Expand by work shape, not by department headcount.** Add the next pocket of Builders, not "the rest of finance."

That turns "a giant seat count, fingers crossed" into "we earned our way up from 100, and every added seat passed a bar."
