Microsoft Copilot · Licensing & Deployment

Copilot, without the guesswork.

Most teams already believe Copilot can help. What stalls them is the licensing maze, the fear of exposing patient data, and the worry that they will hand staff a pile of tools nobody asked for. This guide fixes all three.

A lot of healthcare organizations want Copilot's productivity but get stuck on who gets what, what it costs, and whether it is safe. This guide maps the right Copilot to each role, makes the spend predictable, and lays out a rollout that earns adoption, so you get outcomes without overwhelming your people or exposing PHI.
Where governance starts How to assign licenses Who gets premium and Cowork How consumption is billed Who builds and uses agents HIPAA and PHI guardrails

The whole guide in seven lines

  1. Govern first. Decide who owns AI, what data is in bounds, and where the guardrails are before you buy a single seat.
  2. There are two licenses that matter: Copilot Chat (free, already in your M365) and Microsoft 365 Copilot (the paid seat). "Premium" is not a product you buy.
  3. Give free Chat to everyone eligible. Buy paid seats for the roles where the work is heavy: leaders, managers, analysts.
  4. Cowork needs a paid seat plus an admin switch. Agents run across both tiers and are billed by usage: free for most work on a paid seat, metered on free Chat. Neither is a per-person license. Cap the spend.
  5. Paid seats make most agent use free. Free-Chat users pay per interaction. That one fact drives your budget.
  6. Let a governed set of people build agents. Let everyone use the ones you approve.
  7. Roll out in waves and let the data pull the next one. A small paid pilot beats a big-bang launch every time.

Validated against Microsoft documentation current as of July 21, 2026. Prices that Microsoft renders dynamically are flagged "confirm live" rather than guessed.

01 · The map

Two tiers, one usage layer. That is the whole model.

Before you decide who gets what, get the shape of the product straight. Microsoft's naming has changed a lot, and the confusion costs real money when people buy the wrong thing. Here is the honest version.

Tier 1 · The front door

Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat

Included with your M365. No added per-user cost.

Protected AI chat grounded in the web, with enterprise data protection. Users can upload a file or paste content to work with it. Safe to give broadly once you have handled the web-search path for regulated data (see the Healthcare section). It builds the habit at zero added seat cost. It does not automatically see your organization's data.

Tier 2 · The paid seat

Microsoft 365 Copilot

Add-on, about $30 / user / month (confirm live)

The full assistant. It grounds on your own data through Microsoft Graph (email, meetings, files, chats), edits inside Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook and Teams, and adds the Researcher and Analyst agents, Copilot Search across your data, and SharePoint Advanced Management for governance. It also makes most agent usage free for that person.

Usage layer · Billed by consumption

Copilot Cowork & agents

Billed by usage in Copilot Credits

Cowork runs multi-step work on its own and needs a paid seat plus an admin switch. Agents answer and act on your behalf, and they run across both tiers. Neither is a per-person license. A paid seat makes most agent use free; on free Chat you pay per interaction.

The "premium" trap

There is no "Copilot Premium" to buy for your enterprise. When someone sees M365 Copilot (Premium) inside Word or Excel, that label just means they hold the paid add-on. The $20 consumer "Copilot Pro" is a personal product for home accounts, not this. When you talk to your team, use two names only: Copilot Chat (free) and Microsoft 365 Copilot (paid). It removes 80 percent of the confusion.

Where agents fit

Agents are not a fourth product. They are a capability that runs across both tiers. A paid seat gets the full set (the Agent Store, custom agents grounded in your data, plus Researcher and Analyst) and pays almost nothing to use them. A free-Chat user can still use agents, but every interaction is metered. The Agents section covers who builds them and who can use them.

02 · Start here

Governance is step zero, not step eight.

For leadersFor IT

The organizations that scale Copilot safely make one move first. They decide who owns AI, what good use looks like, and where the guardrails are before they buy a single seat. Skip it and you are cleaning up in production, which in a health system means an inappropriate disclosure you then have to assess and may have to report. Governance is the ground the rest of this plan stands on.

It has two halves. The people side sets direction and owns the decisions. The platform side is the toolkit that makes those decisions real. You need both. One without the other is either a policy nobody enforces or a pile of controls nobody aimed.

The people side: govern before you deploy

Stand up an AI governance committee

One group that owns the AI decisions instead of scattering them across IT tickets. Put the right seats at the table: an executive sponsor, IT and security, compliance and privacy, a clinical or business-unit voice, and HR or change management. What they own: acceptable use, which data is in bounds, who gets which license, how new agents get approved, and a standing risk review. Give it a real cadence, not a one-time kickoff.

Put AI in your mission and strategy

If leadership treats Copilot as a tool IT bought, it stalls. If leadership frames it as how the organization works now, adoption follows. Name AI in the strategy, tie it to outcomes the organization already cares about (clinician time back, lower cost, a better experience for staff and patients), and make it a leadership talking point, not an IT project.

Drive culture from the top

Change management is a leadership job, not a training deck. Executives set the tone by using it themselves, naming the behavior they want to see, and giving people time to build the habit. Top-down permission is what turns "we have licenses" into "this is how we work."

Build a champion network

Champions are your bottom-up engine, and they pair with the top-down sponsorship. A handful of enthusiastic peers in each department, modeling real use and answering questions, moves adoption further than any mandate. Microsoft's Copilot Success Kit and Copilot Academy give them the material to run with.

The sequence that works

Executive sponsorship and the committee come first. Champions carry it the last mile. Do them in that order. Champions with no air cover burn out, and sponsorship that never reaches the floor changes nothing.

The platform side: the controls that make governance real

This is the toolkit your committee's decisions run on. SharePoint Advanced Management comes with your paid Copilot seats. The Purview pieces depend on your broader data-governance licensing, since several capabilities need Microsoft 365 E5 or a Purview add-on, so check what you already hold.

  • The Microsoft 365 admin center is the control plane. Manage who gets Copilot and Chat, control public web access, and manage agents from one place.
  • SharePoint Advanced Management comes with your paid Copilot seat. This is the one people miss. It is included, and it is where you fix the oversharing problem before it becomes an agent problem. Use its Data Access Governance reports to find broadly shared and externally shared sites, and use Restricted Content Discovery (RCD) to cut accidental tenant-wide discovery of sensitive sites while you clean up the permissions. RCD reduces discoverability, it is not an access control, so pair it with permission cleanup, labels, and DLP.
  • Microsoft Purview is your data governance layer. Sensitivity labels that Copilot and agents honor, DSPM for AI to spot risky usage and oversharing, plus audit, eDiscovery, and insider-risk coverage across prompts and responses.
  • Agent 365 and the Agent Registry govern the agents. More on this in the Agents section. The short version: one inventory across your agent platforms, with the power to observe, approve, block, and secure them.
  • Least privilege by default. Use the AI Administrator role to run agent governance, not Global Admin.

Where governance starts in the timeline

Before you buy seats: stand up the committee and name the executive sponsor. Before you turn Copilot on: do the SharePoint Advanced Management and oversharing work and set your labels. Before you scale agents: stand up the Agent Registry and your approval path. Governance is not a phase you reach later. It is the ground you build on from day one.

03 · The centerpiece

Who gets what, by role.

This is the question every customer asks and the one that decides your budget. Pick a role to see the recommended tier, what comes with the seat, and what costs extra. The full comparison table sits below for a side-by-side view.

Director / Executive

Paid seat

High meeting load, long email threads, and constant cross-document synthesis. This is the fastest measurable payback for a paid seat, so fund it first.

Comes with the seat

  • Meeting recaps and action items across a busy calendar
  • Outlook thread summaries and drafting grounded in real context
  • "What did we decide about X in the last 30 days?" across their files
  • Researcher and Analyst for board and strategy prep

Costs extra

  • Cowork, if they want it to run multi-step work on its own (usage-billed)
  • Custom agents they use are mostly free on a paid seat

Healthcare example: a VP of Operations walks into a system leadership meeting with a synthesized read on last month's staffing, budget, and project threads, prepared in minutes.

Manager / Project lead

Paid seat

Meetings, team coordination, and status roll-ups. Strong, reliable payback. Managers also become your best champions because they model the behavior for their teams.

Comes with the seat

  • Catch up on missed meetings and share the recap with the team
  • Draft status updates and project briefs from notes and email
  • "What did my team commit to this week?"
  • Dashboard access if enabled for them; team-level views depend on tenant setup

Costs extra

  • Cowork for recurring multi-step workflows (usage-billed)

Healthcare example: a nurse manager turns a week of shift notes and emails into a clean handoff summary instead of staying late to write it.

Analyst (Finance, Data, Research, Ops)

Paid seat

Excel and data work show some of the highest measured returns. If you only had ten paid seats, a chunk of them belong here.

Comes with the seat

  • Excel formula help, chart suggestions, and insights on a dataset
  • The Excel, Word, and PowerPoint agents for deeper data-to-deliverable work
  • Analyst agent for structured reasoning over data
  • Data-to-presentation in PowerPoint

Costs extra

  • Heavy Cowork usage for large multi-source aggregation
  • Copilot Studio capacity if they build shared agents
  • Azure consumption if they move to custom Foundry agents

Healthcare example: a revenue-cycle analyst asks Copilot to find denial patterns across a claims export and draft the summary for the finance review.

General knowledge / administrative worker

Start on Chat Graduate when ready

The largest population and the place people overspend. Start them on free Chat. Watch the dashboard. Move the ones with real, repeated use up to a paid seat. Do not buy seats on hope.

Comes with free Chat

  • Web research, drafting, and analysis on uploaded files
  • Meeting prep and writing help
  • Approved pay-as-you-go agents (metered)

What the paid seat adds

  • Grounding in their own email, files, and meetings
  • In-app editing in Word and Outlook
  • "What did I miss while I was out?" across their own data

Healthcare example: a scheduling coordinator uses free Chat for drafting and quick web research, and earns a paid seat once the team leans on it daily.

Engineer / Developer

GitHub Copilot + paid seat for collab

Two different tools. GitHub Copilot handles code and is licensed separately. A Microsoft 365 Copilot seat handles the meetings, docs, and email around the code. If they build agents, that is a third path (see Agents).

What they use

  • GitHub Copilot in the IDE for code (separate license)
  • M365 Copilot for sprint recaps, technical docs, and thread digests
  • Copilot Studio or Azure AI Foundry to build custom agents

Costs extra

  • Copilot Studio user license plus tenant capacity for makers
  • Azure consumption (tokens and infra) for Foundry-built agents

Healthcare example: an integration engineer builds a Foundry agent against an internal API, billed through Azure, while using a paid seat for the project's meetings and documentation.

Frontline worker (F-plans)

Chat if eligible

Shift-based and deskless staff. Free Chat is the right on-ramp where their plan qualifies. A paid seat can be added to F1 or F3 for the specific frontline roles that do enough knowledge work to justify it.

Comes with the seat

  • Free Chat for quick answers and drafting on eligible plans
  • Paid Copilot can layer onto F1 / F3 for select roles

Confirm before you assume

  • Cowork on frontline plans is not clearly documented. Verify with your account team before you promise it.

Healthcare example: a unit clerk uses Chat for quick answers and message drafting on a shared device, without a paid seat.

Security / Compliance team

Security Copilot

A different product for a different job. Security Copilot is purpose-built for threat hunting and incident response and plugs into Defender, Purview, Entra, and Intune. Your compliance leads also own the guardrails for everyone else's agents.

What they use

  • Microsoft Security Copilot for SOC work (separate product)
  • Purview and the admin center to govern all Copilot and agent activity

Their cross-cutting job

  • Sensitivity labels, DSPM for AI, and the Agent Registry for the whole org

Healthcare example: the compliance officer sets label policies so agents never surface PHI-marked files to people who should not summarize them.

04 · Lens one

The executive approach.

For leaders

If you are the executive sponsor, your job is not to pick features. It is to fund the right populations, set the guardrails, and protect the rollout from turning into noise. Five moves.

1. Fund the front door for everyone

Give Copilot Chat to every eligible employee. It is already in your M365 subscription, it carries enterprise data protection, and it costs nothing extra per user. This is how you build the AI habit across the organization without a budget fight.

2. Buy paid seats where the work is heavy

Meetings, email volume, and spreadsheet-heavy analysis are where a paid seat pays for itself fastest. That points at leaders, managers, and analysts first. Resist spreading a thin layer of paid seats across everyone. A focused cohort produces visible wins you can point to.

3. Treat Cowork and agents as a metered budget

These are consumption lines, not headcount lines. Turn them on for the roles that need them, set tenant, group, and per-user spend limits, and watch the usage reports. Prepaid capacity gives you a hard ceiling; pay-as-you-go keeps billing past your alerts, so set the alerts and per-agent caps on purpose.

4. Measure, then expand

The Copilot Dashboard shows you who is engaged and surfaces candidates for a paid seat. Let that data pull your next wave of licenses. Buying seats ahead of demonstrated use is how pilots stall and budgets get questioned.

5. Do not overwhelm your people

The fastest way to kill adoption is to drop six new capabilities on a team in one week. Start with one or two habits that solve a real daily annoyance. Let people get a win before you add the next thing.

The sound bite for your leadership team

"Free Copilot for everyone, paid seats for the roles that live in meetings and spreadsheets, and agents turned on with a spending cap. We expand based on what the data shows, not what people ask for."

What it costs you to get this wrong

Buy paid seats for everyone and you burn budget on people who barely touch it. Skip the front door and you never build the habit. Turn agents loose without governance in a health system and you risk an agent surfacing PHI it should not. None of these are hypothetical. All three are avoidable with the plan in this guide.

05 · Lens two

The IT deployment playbook.

For IT

Microsoft's own guidance breaks deployment into five steps. Here they are with the mechanics that matter, including the part everyone asks about: how you actually assign the licenses.

1

Get the org ready, starting with governance

Stand up the governance foundation from section 02 first: the committee, the executive sponsor, and the guardrails. Then run the Microsoft 365 Copilot Optimization Assessment to benchmark your data governance, and fix oversharing before you turn anything on, because Copilot only surfaces what a user can already reach, and in most tenants that is more than people think. Turn on the security basics: MFA, audit logging, and sensitivity labels.

2

Choose the license per population

Use the role matrix. Decide who gets free Chat (everyone eligible) and who gets a paid seat (the high-ROI roles). Write it down as named groups before you buy anything.

3

Ready the apps and network

Put Microsoft 365 Apps on the Current or Monthly Enterprise channel, confirm Teams, Outlook, and OneDrive are configured, and allow the required endpoints. This is quick, but skipping it produces the "why doesn't Copilot show up" tickets.

4

Assign licenses with groups, not one by one

Use group-based licensing in the Microsoft 365 admin center. Create a security group per population, assign the license to the group, and let membership drive who has a seat. It scales, it is auditable, and it makes de-provisioning clean.

5

Enable users and give them a reason

Send the welcome email, point people at Copilot Academy and the Prompt Gallery, and name your champions. A license nobody was taught to use is wasted money.

Deep dive: how to structure your licensing groups

Create groups that map to the matrix, and keep the naming obvious so anyone auditing the tenant can read your intent:

  • Copilot-Chat-All-Eligible: Chat is already included for eligible users, so use a group to manage its availability and policies, not to assign a license. This is your free front door.
  • Copilot-Paid-Leadership, Copilot-Paid-Finance-Ops, Copilot-Paid-Analysts: paid add-on seats, sliced by business unit so you can track ROI per unit.
  • Copilot-Cowork-Enabled and CopilotStudio-Makers: small, deliberately governed groups for the usage-billed and build capabilities.

Membership can be static or dynamic (rule-based on department or job title). Dynamic groups keep the paid population honest as people move roles. You can also assign licenses with PowerShell or Microsoft Graph if you are automating from an identity system.

Do the oversharing work before you scale, not after

In a health system this is the difference between a clean rollout and a compliance incident. Use SharePoint Advanced Management (included with a paid Copilot seat) to find broadly shared sites, apply Restricted Content Discovery to sensitive sites, and label PHI content in Purview so Copilot and agents respect it. Details are in the Healthcare section.

How you measure it

The Microsoft Copilot Dashboard in Viva Insights is your instrument panel. It needs no extra license, works with as few as one Copilot seat, and unlocks the full picture at 50 seats. It shows readiness (who is a prime candidate for a paid seat), adoption (active use by app and by group), and impact (assisted hours and value, which are modeled estimates, not audited savings). Use it to justify the next wave of spend with numbers instead of anecdotes.

06 · The money question

Consumption without surprises.

For leadersFor IT

This is where customers get nervous, and it is simpler than it looks once you hold one fact in your head: if you own the paid seat, most of your everyday agent use is already paid for. If you are on free Chat, every agent answer is a metered charge.

The currency: Copilot Credits

Agent and Cowork usage is billed in Copilot Credits. Microsoft renamed these from "messages" on September 1, 2025 with no change to the rate. Pay-as-you-go runs about $0.01 per credit through an Azure subscription. Different actions cost different amounts of credits.

ActionCreditsRough cost (PAYG)
Classic answer1~$0.01
Generative answer2~$0.02
Agent action5~$0.05
Grounding on your tenant data (per response)10~$0.10
Generative answer grounded in your data12 (10 + 2)~$0.12

The fact that drives your budget

For a user with a paid Microsoft 365 Copilot seat, everyday employee-facing agent use (classic answers, generative answers, agent actions, and grounding on your own data) is zero-rated. It does not draw down credits, within fair-use limits. For a user on free Chat, those same interactions are metered at the rates above. So the paid seat is not only a productivity upgrade, it also flattens your agent bill for that person.

Prepaid or pay-as-you-go

You have two ways to fund the metered usage. Prepaid capacity packs give you a fixed monthly ceiling and no billing surprises (the pack structure has historically been about $200 for 25,000 credits, confirm current pricing). Pay-as-you-go bills in arrears through Azure and suits variable or low volume. Most organizations use prepaid for the predictable base load and a pay-as-you-go plan as an overflow safety net.

Your cost controls

  • Per-agent monthly credit caps, so a single runaway agent cannot drain the pool.
  • Automatic enforcement: agent flows stop at 100 percent of prepaid capacity, and custom agents are disabled at 125 percent. You get email and admin-center alerts before that.
  • Tenant, group, and per-user spend limits for Cowork, with configurable usage alerts.
  • The Copilot Studio Agent Usage Estimator to forecast before you deploy at scale, and Azure budgets for anything running in Foundry.
How Azure AI Foundry agents bill differently

Foundry does not use Copilot Credits. It bills the way Azure always has: per token for the model calls, plus any tools (search, file search, code interpreter), plus infrastructure and, for hosted agents, container compute. There is no separate "agent service" fee. Use Azure Cost Management, budgets, and tags for department-level tracking, and reserve provisioned throughput only for predictable, high-volume workloads. Think of Copilot Studio as abstracted, credit-based billing tied to your M365 licensing, and Foundry as direct, token-level billing with full control.

The budgeting sound bite: "Paid seats make most of our everyday agent use free. We only pay real consumption for customer-facing agents, for free-Chat users, and for Cowork, and we hold those with limits and alerts."

07 · The part people fear most

Agents: who builds them, who uses them, and who governs them.

For ITFor leaders

Three questions live here, and mixing them up is what makes agents feel scary. Who is allowed to build an agent. Who is allowed to use one. And how you govern the whole set. Answer them separately and the fear goes away.

Who can build: three surfaces, three audiences

Build surfaceWho it is forWhat it takes to buildWhat you can build

Who can use: it follows the license

  • Paid Microsoft 365 Copilot seat: full access to the Agent Store, custom agents grounded in your data, and the Researcher and Analyst agents, with most usage zero-rated.
  • Free Chat: can use approved pay-as-you-go agents, metered per interaction.
  • Sharing: a maker shares an agent with named people or security groups, with the whole org, or publishes it to your catalog (which requires admin approval). Someone using a shared Copilot Studio agent does not need a Studio license. The usage bills to the tenant.
  • Foundry-built agents: access is granted with the Foundry Agent Consumer role, and it can be scoped down to an individual agent.

How you keep control

This is the answer to "won't agents get out of hand." No, if you set the guardrails first.

  • The Agent Registry in the Microsoft 365 admin center gives you one inventory across platforms (Microsoft, partner, and homegrown from Copilot Studio, Agent Builder, SharePoint, and Foundry). You can block, allow, and approve from there. Draft-agent visibility still varies by platform, so keep the platform-level views too. Agent 365 is the broader control plane above it.
  • Use the AI Administrator role, not Global Admin, to govern agents. Least privilege by default.
  • Power Platform DLP lets you force every agent to require sign-in, block risky knowledge connectors, route makers into managed environments, and even disable generative-AI agent publishing until a review is done.
  • Purview carries the data side: sensitivity labels the agents honor, DSPM for AI to spot oversharing, plus audit, eDiscovery, and insider-risk coverage for prompts and responses.

Agent 365: the control plane when agents scale

Once agents multiply past a handful, you want one place to see and manage agents across your platforms. That is Agent 365. Think of it as the layer above the Agent Registry, built on three jobs: observe (one map of agents from supported Microsoft and connected third-party platforms), govern (give agents identity, access, approval, and a lifecycle the same way you manage people), and secure (protect against agent-specific risks). It went generally available on May 1, 2026.

The honest read for planning: Agent 365 is a newer layer that licenses per user, so people who build, manage, or interact with agents through its premium capabilities may need a license. It works best on a Microsoft 365 E5 foundation and is bundled into the newer Microsoft 365 E7. You do not need it to start. The Agent Registry and Purview cover you for a first wave of agents. Add Agent 365 when your agent count, or your risk, grows past what the admin center alone should carry. Confirm current licensing and healthcare BAA coverage with your Microsoft account team before you commit.

The posture to recommend

Let everyone use the agents you approve. Let a governed few build. Business users build simple agents in Agent Builder. IT and pro developers build the serious ones in Copilot Studio and Foundry. The Agent Registry plus DLP, with Agent 365 as you scale, is the seatbelt that makes "let people build" safe to say out loud.

08 · The non-negotiable

Healthcare and compliance.

HealthcareFor IT

For a health system this section decides whether the rest of the plan is allowed to happen. The short version: the core products are covered, the risk is oversharing, and there are three dated items you need to handle this month.

What is covered under your BAA

Microsoft 365 Copilot and Copilot Chat are both explicitly in scope for HIPAA, and the Business Associate Agreement comes through the Microsoft Data Protection Addendum by default. You do not sign a separate contract. Azure and Azure OpenAI (Microsoft-operated) are covered through the Azure BAA. Your prompts and responses are not used to train the foundation models.

The web-search carve-out

HIPAA coverage does not extend to Copilot's public web-search queries, because those run under different terms. Keep PHI out of web-grounded prompts, or have an admin disable web search for regulated groups. This is the single most common miss in healthcare Copilot rollouts.

Three dated items to handle now

July 24, 2026: OpenAI-operated models become enabled by default for eligible commercial customers unless you set OpenAI to "No users" first. They do not carry HITRUST, SOC 1, or FedRAMP High. If your risk framework requires any of those for AI processing, disable them in the admin center before that date. Azure OpenAI (Microsoft-operated) keeps HITRUST and FedRAMP High and is unaffected.

July 31, 2026: new enablement of Restricted SharePoint Search is blocked. Move your oversharing control to Restricted Content Discovery through SharePoint Advanced Management.

Ongoing: Anthropic, which powers Cowork and Researcher, sits outside the EU Data Boundary and is a named subprocessor. Confirm BAA coverage with your Microsoft account team before you turn on Cowork or Researcher for PHI-adjacent work.

Oversharing is the real risk, so control it

When many people can build agents, the danger is not the model, it is an agent surfacing PHI from a SharePoint site that was shared too broadly years ago. The tools live in the governance section. SharePoint Advanced Management comes with your paid Copilot seat; the Purview pieces depend on your data-governance licensing. For PHI specifically, lean on these:

  • Restricted Content Discovery to cut accidental tenant-wide discovery of clinical and sensitive sites while you run the permissions cleanup. It reduces discoverability, it does not replace fixing the permissions.
  • Data Access Governance reports in SharePoint Advanced Management to find the overshared sites before an agent does.
  • Sensitivity labels on PHI content, which Copilot and agents honor and which require the right usage rights to surface.

Know the product boundary

Microsoft draws a hard line between two tracks, and it is worth stating clearly:

  • Microsoft 365 Copilot is for knowledge and administrative workers: operations, revenue cycle, HR, IT, compliance, and non-clinical management. That is what this guide is about.
  • Dragon Copilot is the separate product for clinicians doing ambient clinical documentation (the successor to DAX). Different license, different infrastructure, different rollout. Neither is a medical device.

One more for the highest-security environments: Agent Builder does not support Customer-Managed Keys. If your risk tier requires you to hold your own encryption key, build those agents in Copilot Studio instead.

09 · Make it stick

Roll it out without overwhelming the team.

For leadersFor IT

The worry comes up in every rollout: do not flood the team. It is a good instinct. Adoption is a habit you build in waves, not a launch you announce once. Here is a shape that works.

Before Phase 1: the governance foundation from section 02 should already be standing. The committee, the executive sponsor, and the oversharing cleanup are the ground these waves run on, not a task inside them.

Phase 1 · Prove

Weeks 1 to 4

  • Free Chat to a broad eligible group
  • Paid seats to a small, high-ROI cohort (leaders, a few analysts)
  • Name 3 to 5 champions
  • Teach one or two daily habits, nothing more
Phase 2 · Expand

Weeks 5 to 10

  • Use the dashboard to graduate engaged Chat users to paid seats
  • Ship one governed agent that solves a real annoyance
  • Share modeled assisted-hours estimates with leadership
  • Turn on Cowork for a couple of power users, with a spend cap
Phase 3 · Scale

Weeks 11+

  • Expand department by department, not all at once
  • Open agent building to governed makers
  • Roll Cowork to the roles that earn it
  • Review usage and spend on a monthly cadence

What keeps it from stalling

  • Champions carry it, not IT. Peers modeling real use beats any training deck.
  • One habit at a time. Meeting recaps this month. Email triage next. Let each win land before the next ask.
  • Measure and show. The dashboard turns "I think it's helping" into a modeled estimate of the hours you got back. That is what funds the next wave.

The rollout sound bite: "We are not launching Copilot to everyone on Monday. We are building a habit in waves, proving the value with data, and letting demand pull the next group in."

  1. Stand up your AI governance committee and name the executive sponsor. This is step zero, before any spend.
  2. Turn on free Chat for everyone eligible (it is already included), and handle the web-search path for PHI-heavy groups first.
  3. Buy a focused block of paid seats (start in the dozens, not the thousands) for leaders, managers, and analysts.
  4. Do the oversharing work with SharePoint Advanced Management, and stand up the Agent Registry before you scale agents.
  5. Handle the three dated compliance items in the Healthcare section, the July 24 one first.

Not ready to buy yet? Run the Microsoft 365 Copilot Optimization Assessment and walk your leadership through the role matrix above. That is a real first step with no spend attached.
